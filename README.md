## Preamble

This license exists because the law hasn't caught up with reality.

Traditional open-source licenses (MIT, BSD, GPL, Apache) are built on a single
foundation: **copyright**. The licensor owns a copyright in the work and grants
you permission to use it under certain terms. Remove the copyright and the
entire mechanism collapses — there is nothing to license, nothing to grant,
nothing to restrict.

The copyrightability of AI-generated works is unsettled and jurisdiction-
dependent. In the United States, the Copyright Office evaluates AI-involved
works case-by-case, focusing on whether a human exercised sufficient control
over the expressive elements; purely AI-generated output without such human
authorship has been denied registration. In the UK, statute provides that for
certain "computer-generated works," the author is the person who made the
necessary arrangements. Other jurisdictions are still developing their
positions. This means a growing volume of functional, deployable software
exists in a legal patchwork: potentially ownable in one country, potentially
unownable in another, and carrying no formal warranty or liability framework
in either.

**Slop0BSD** is designed for this uncertainty. It is a hybrid instrument — part
waiver, part license, part contract — for code, specifications, documentation,
and other works that are substantially or entirely generated by AI systems.

It operates in three ordered layers:

1. **Waiver.** To the maximum extent permitted by law, the Distributor waives
   all copyright, related rights, and moral rights in the Work. Where moral
   rights cannot be waived, the Distributor covenants not to assert them.

2. **Fallback License.** To the extent any rights survive that cannot be
   waived or rendered unassertable, the Distributor grants an unconditional,
   irrevocable, perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free license to use, copy,
   modify, and distribute the Work for any purpose.

3. **Contractual Terms.** The Distributor makes a standing offer of warranty
   disclaimers and liability limitations to any Recipient who preserves the
   Provenance Declaration. Acceptance is by the act of preservation and
   redistribution. These terms are enforceable under contract law,
   independent of copyright.

The name is intentional. "Slop" is the colloquial term for AI-generated
content. We don't run from it. We label it honestly so downstream consumers
can make informed decisions.

## Definitions

**"AI System"** means any machine learning model, large language model, code
generation tool, or automated system that produces output without direct
character-by-character human authorship. This includes but is not limited to
systems such as ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Gemini, Codex, open-weight models,
and local inference engines.

**"AI-Generated"** means the Work was produced primarily or entirely by an AI
System, where human involvement was limited to providing prompts, selecting
among outputs, or performing minor edits that may not constitute sufficient
original authorship under applicable copyright law.

**"AI-Assisted"** means a human author made substantial creative, architectural,
or expressive decisions, using an AI System as a tool — analogous to using a
compiler, IDE autocomplete, or reference manual. The human contribution is the
dominant creative element.

**"Distributor"** means the person or entity that makes the Work available
under this license. The Distributor is always a legal person or entity —
never "None."

**"Recipient"** means any person or entity that receives, uses, copies,
modifies, or distributes the Work.

**"Provenance Declaration"** means the structured metadata file describing the
authorship composition of the Work. The canonical format is a YAML file named
`SLOP0BSD.provenance` at the repository root, conforming to the schema defined
in the Slop0BSD Specification. An equivalent machine-readable format (JSON,
TOML, or embedded in package metadata) satisfies this definition provided it
contains the required fields.

**"The Work"** means any source code, object code, specifications, documentation,
configuration, or other material distributed under this license.

## Provenance Declaration

Every project distributed under Slop0BSD **SHOULD** include a Provenance
Declaration. The canonical filename is `SLOP0BSD.provenance` at the repository
root.

### Schema

```yaml
# SLOP0BSD Provenance Declaration
license: Slop0BSD-0.4.0

provenance:
  # Required. One of: ai-generated | ai-assisted | mixed | human-authored
  authorship: ai-generated

  # Required. The legal entity distributing the Work.
  distributor: "Jane Doe <jane@example.com>"

  # Required. The AI system(s) used.
  generators:
    - name: "Claude"
      version: "Opus 4"
      provider: "Anthropic"

  # Required. Description of human involvement.
  human_role: "Prompting and output selection only"

  # Optional. Percentage estimate of human-authored content (0-100).
  human_content_pct: 5

  # Optional. Origin of the specification or design.
  spec_origin: "ai-generated"

  # Optional. Date of generation (ISO 8601).
  generated: "2026-03-29"

  # Optional. Link to prompt transcript or session log.
  transcript: "https://example.com/session/abc123"

  # Optional. TrustVer-compatible version string.
  trustver: "1.0.0+ai"
```

### Authorship Categories

| Category | Typical Copyright Status | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `ai-generated` | May not be copyrightable (varies by jurisdiction) | AI produced the work; human involvement limited to prompts and selection |
| `ai-assisted` | Likely copyrightable where human contribution is dominant | Human made dominant creative decisions; AI used as a tool |
| `mixed` | Partially copyrightable (human portions) | Identifiable human-authored and AI-generated components |
| `human-authored` | Copyrightable | Traditional human authorship; consider a standard license instead |

## Compatibility & Interaction with Other Licenses

### Downstream Compatibility

Because Slop0BSD first waives copyright (Section 1.1), waives or covenants
against moral rights (Section 1.2), grants a patent license (Section 1.3),
and then grants unconditional fallback permission (Section 2), the Work
carries no conditions that would conflict with downstream licenses. It is
expected to be compatible with MIT, Apache-2.0, GPL, AGPL, and proprietary
licenses. However, Slop0BSD is a custom license that has not been reviewed by
the OSI, is not on the SPDX license list, and has not been tested in court.
Risk-averse adopters should evaluate compatibility for their specific use case
with legal counsel.

### Patent Considerations

Section 1.3 provides an explicit patent grant from the Distributor, scoped to
claims necessarily infringed by the Work alone (not combinations). This is
narrower than Apache-2.0's patent grant and does not cover third-party patent
claims. Recipients who are integrating Slop0BSD code into patent-sensitive
environments should conduct independent patent due diligence.

### Upstream Contamination

If a human wrote code under a separate license (e.g., MIT) and an AI system
subsequently modifies it, the human's original contribution retains its
original copyright and license. The AI-generated modifications may not be
copyrightable, but the composite work carries the most restrictive
applicable license from its copyrightable components. Slop0BSD does not
override pre-existing copyright.

### Copyleft Interaction

Material that is not copyrightable cannot be subject to copyleft conditions,
because there is no copyright to enforce the copyleft mechanism. To the
extent that AI-generated Slop0BSD code is not copyrightable in a given
jurisdiction, incorporating it into a GPL project would not GPL-encumber
that portion. The copyleft would apply only to copyrightable portions of the
composite work.

**Note:** This is the authors' legal analysis and has not been tested in
court. The interaction between non-copyrightable material and copyleft
licenses is an open question. Consult legal counsel for high-stakes
applications.

## Rationale & Legal Basis

### The Problem

Traditional open-source licenses assume a single legal reality: the work is
copyrighted, and the author grants you permission to use it. But AI-generated
works create (at least) three possible realities:

1. The work is copyrightable (because a human exercised sufficient creative
   control over the expressive elements).
2. The work may not be copyrightable (because it is primarily AI-generated
   without sufficient human authorship).
3. The copyright status is unclear, contested, or jurisdiction-dependent.

A license that only handles scenario (1) is incomplete. A public-domain
dedication that only handles scenario (2) provides no contractual protections.
Slop0BSD is designed to operate across all three.

### Prior Art

**CC0 1.0 Universal** pioneered the waiver-then-fallback structure. Slop0BSD
adapts this pattern for software and adds the contractual provenance layer
that CC0 lacks. CC0 was designed for a world where copyright exists and
the author wants to disclaim it. Slop0BSD is designed for a world where
copyright may never have existed in the first place.

**0BSD** is a maximally permissive license but asserts copyright in its
header ("Copyright (c) ..."). For works where the copyright status is
uncertain, this assertion may be inaccurate. Slop0BSD replaces the copyright
assertion with a Distributor declaration.

**Apache-2.0** provides an explicit patent grant with retaliation clause.
Section 1.3 of Slop0BSD adapts this model with a narrower scope appropriate
to AI-generated works where the patent landscape is often uncertain.

### Key Authorities (United States)

- **Thaler v. Perlmutter (D.D.C. 2023):** Held that a work autonomously
  generated by AI without human authorship does not satisfy the Copyright
  Act's requirement of human authorship.
- **U.S. Copyright Office, Registration Guidance (Feb 2023, updated 2025):**
  Evaluates AI-involved works case-by-case; copyrightability depends on
  whether a human exercised sufficient creative control over the expressive
  elements. Purely AI-generated output has been denied registration, but
  AI-assisted works with sufficient human authorship may qualify.
- **Kashtanova / Zarya of the Dawn (2023):** Individual AI-generated images
  denied copyright; human-selected arrangement and text granted thin copyright.
- **ProCD v. Zeidenberg (7th Cir. 1996):** Shrinkwrap contracts enforceable
  where there is notice and opportunity to reject.
- **Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893):** Unilateral offers accepted
  by performance are enforceable contracts. Foundational authority for
  Section 3's acceptance-by-preservation mechanism.
- **Nguyen v. Barnes & Noble (9th Cir. 2014):** Browsewrap agreements
  unenforceable without clear notice and unambiguous assent. Motivates
  Section 3's explicit acceptance-by-performance mechanism rather than
  implied acceptance-by-use.

### Key Authorities (International)

- **UK CDPA 1988, §9(3):** Provides that for certain "computer-generated
  works," the author is "the person by whom the arrangements necessary for
  the creation of the work are made." This may extend copyright to some
  AI-generated works under UK law, making Sections 1 and 2 operative.
- **French Code de la propriété intellectuelle, Art. L121-1:** Moral rights
  are perpetual, inalienable, and imprescriptible. Section 1.2's non-assertion
  covenant addresses this by binding the Distributor contractually rather than
  attempting to waive the right itself.
- **German Urheberrechtsgesetz (UrhG), §11 et seq.:** Copyright is
  inseparable from the author's person. Moral rights are similarly
  inalienable. Section 1.2's covenant approach applies here as well.
- **EU AI Act, Regulation 2024/1689, Art. 50:** Imposes transparency
  obligations for specific categories of AI systems and specific types of
  AI-generated content (including synthetic audio, image, video, and text
  published to inform the public on matters of public interest). The Act
  does not impose a general provenance labeling requirement on all
  AI-generated software. Slop0BSD's Provenance Declaration is a voluntary
  transparency measure that may assist with compliance where Art. 50
  obligations apply, but is not required by the Act for all use cases.

## Design Decisions

### Why the Distributor, Not the Author?

Traditional licenses name the copyright author as the grantor. But for
AI-generated works:
- The AI system cannot be a legal person.
- The prompter may not have contributed enough for authorship.
- "Copyright (c) None" is legally meaningless — no person is making the grant.

Slop0BSD names the **Distributor** — the person or entity who packages and
releases the Work — as the operative party. The Distributor is always a real
legal entity who can waive rights (Section 1), grant licenses (Section 2),
and make contractual offers (Section 3).

### Why a Unilateral Offer, Not a Bilateral Contract?

Earlier drafts framed Section 3 as a bilateral contract triggered by
"exercising any rights in the Work." Review identified a critical flaw: if the
Work is not copyrightable, the Recipient isn't exercising rights granted by
the Distributor — they're exercising rights that exist because of the absence
of copyright. The *Nguyen v. Barnes & Noble* line of cases further
demonstrates that courts will reject terms that lack clear assent.

Slop0BSD reframes Section 3 as a **unilateral offer accepted by performance**.
The Distributor offers warranty disclaimers and liability limitations to any
Recipient who preserves provenance. The Recipient accepts by the act of
preserving and redistributing the Provenance Declaration. This solves the
formation problem because:

- The Distributor's consideration is the provenance infrastructure itself:
  curated, packaged, documented code with a machine-readable authorship
  declaration.
- The Recipient's consideration is the act of preservation: maintaining the
  provenance chain for downstream consumers.
- The exchange of value does not depend on copyright existing.
- A Recipient who strips provenance is not breaching a contract — they were
  never party to one. They simply receive no contractual protections.

This follows *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.* (1893), the foundational
authority for unilateral contracts accepted by performance.

### Why a Moral Rights Non-Assertion Covenant?

Civil law jurisdictions (France, Germany, and others) treat moral rights as
inalienable. A license that only says "moral rights are waived to the extent
waivable" leaves a gap in these jurisdictions — the rights survive and could
theoretically be asserted.

Section 1.2 closes this gap with a **non-assertion covenant**: even where
the rights cannot be extinguished, the Distributor contractually promises
not to use them. The covenant binds successors, heirs, and assigns. This is
a well-established pattern in IP licensing (common in patent non-assertion
covenants) adapted for moral rights.

**Limitation:** A non-assertion covenant is a contractual obligation, not
an extinction of the right. A court in a strong moral-rights jurisdiction
could find the covenant unenforceable as contrary to public policy. Section 5
(Severability) ensures this does not collapse the rest of the instrument.

### Why an Explicit Patent Clause?

Earlier drafts addressed only copyright and moral rights, creating a gap
for patent claims. For AI-generated code, the Distributor may hold process
patents, and silence on patent rights creates ambiguity.

Section 1.3 provides a narrow, explicit patent grant modeled on Apache-2.0's
approach: it covers claims necessarily infringed by the Work alone, includes
a retaliation clause (patent litigation terminates the grant), and makes no
representation about third-party patents. This gives downstream consumers
clarity without overpromising.

### Why Incentive-Based Provenance, Not Mandatory Retention?

Traditional licenses use copyright to enforce conditions ("you must retain
this notice"). If the work isn't copyrightable, that enforcement mechanism
is absent. Requiring retention without a mechanism to enforce it creates a
false obligation that erodes trust in the license.

Slop0BSD uses a **conditional benefit**: the warranty disclaimer and
liability limitation in Section 3 protect only distributions that preserve
provenance. Strip the provenance, and you redistribute "naked" — with full
exposure to warranty and liability claims. This is self-enforcing because:

- It doesn't depend on copyright for enforcement.
- It creates a natural economic incentive to preserve provenance.
- It doesn't create unenforceable mandates or false obligations.
- Downstream redistributors make a rational choice, not a coerced one.
- The unilateral-offer framing means non-preservers aren't "violating"
  anything — they simply aren't party to the contract.

### Why Scoped Provenance Liability?

Section 3.3(c) creates liability for knowingly falsifying provenance, but
scopes it narrowly:
- **Mens rea:** Requires knowledge ("knowingly makes a material
  falsification") — innocent mistakes are not actionable.
- **Materiality:** Only material falsifications trigger liability — trivial
  inaccuracies do not.
- **Causation:** Liability is for losses "proximately caused by reasonable
  reliance" — speculative or remote damages are excluded.
- **Jurisdictional respect:** Capped by "to the extent permitted by
  applicable law."

This is narrower and more defensible than v0.3's "assumes full liability"
language, which courts might have treated as an unenforceable penalty clause.

## FAQ

**Q: Why not just use 0BSD or MIT?**

A: Because they assert copyright ownership ("Copyright (c) ...") and grant
permission *from* that copyright. If the work's copyright status is uncertain,
those assertions may be inaccurate. Slop0BSD doesn't assume copyright exists —
it handles the full spectrum from copyrightable to non-copyrightable.

**Q: Can someone take my Slop0BSD project and sell it?**

A: Yes. Section 1 waives copyright. Section 2 grants unconditional permission
on any remainder. If the work is not copyrightable, anyone can use it
regardless of this license.

**Q: What if I strip the provenance and redistribute?**

A: You can. You aren't breaching any contract — you were never party to one.
But you lose the warranty disclaimer and liability limitation from Section 3.
If the code causes losses downstream, you have no contractual shield.

**Q: Does the provenance representation create liability for distributors?**

A: Section 3.3(a) is a "best knowledge and belief" representation, not an
absolute warranty. Accurate good-faith disclosure protects you. Section 3.3(c)
creates narrowly scoped liability only for *knowing material falsification*
that *proximately causes* losses to someone who *reasonably relied* on the
false provenance. Innocent mistakes, trivial inaccuracies, and speculative
damages are not actionable.

**Q: What about patents?**

A: Section 1.3 provides an explicit patent grant from the Distributor,
covering claims necessarily infringed by the Work alone. It includes a
retaliation clause: if you sue anyone claiming the Work infringes a patent,
your patent license terminates. The grant does not cover third-party patents
or combinations with other software. If you are integrating Slop0BSD code
into a patent-sensitive product, conduct independent patent due diligence.

**Q: What about jurisdictions where AI works ARE copyrightable?**

A: Section 1.1 waives that copyright. Section 1.2 handles moral rights.
Section 2 provides a fallback license if the waiver is incomplete. Section 3
applies regardless. In the UK under CDPA §9(3), the Distributor who "made the
arrangements" for AI generation may hold copyright — Sections 1 and 2 address
this.

**Q: What about moral rights in France or Germany?**

A: Section 1.2 takes a two-step approach: waive where permitted, then
irrevocably covenant not to assert where waiver is impossible. The covenant
binds successors, heirs, and assigns. This is the same pattern used in patent
non-assertion covenants, adapted for moral rights. Note that enforceability
of this covenant in strong moral-rights jurisdictions has not been tested;
Section 5 (Severability) provides a safety net.

**Q: What if I just use the code without redistributing?**

A: You're fine. Sections 1 and 2 give you unconditional rights to use the
code. You aren't party to the Section 3 contract (no acceptance by
performance occurred), but you don't need it — the warranty disclaimer and
liability limitation primarily protect redistributors.

**Q: Is this license OSI-approved?**

A: No. Slop0BSD is a custom license that has not been submitted to the OSI
or added to the SPDX license list. It includes contractual provisions beyond
the scope of a traditional copyright license. Until listed, use
`LicenseRef-Slop0BSD-0.4.0` in SPDX expressions.

**Q: Is this license production-ready?**

A: Slop0BSD has been developed through multiple rounds of legal review and
iterative refinement, but it has not been reviewed by a software licensing
attorney, is not court-tested, and is not endorsed by any standards body.
For personal, experimental, and community projects, it provides a materially
better framework than applying a traditional copyright license to AI-generated
works. For commercial or enterprise use, have it reviewed by legal counsel.

**Q: Does the EU AI Act require this kind of provenance?**

A: Not generally. The EU AI Act (Art. 50) imposes transparency obligations
for specific categories of AI systems and specific types of AI-generated
content. It does not require provenance labeling on all AI-generated software.
Slop0BSD's Provenance Declaration is a voluntary transparency measure that
goes beyond current legal requirements and may assist with compliance where
Art. 50 obligations are applicable.

## TrustVer Integration

Projects using both Slop0BSD and the [TrustVer](https://thirdkey.ai/trustver)
versioning specification can embed authorship provenance directly in version
strings:

```
1.0.0+ai        # Fully AI-generated
1.2.0+hrai      # Mixed human and AI authorship
2.0.0+hr        # Human-authored (consider a different license)
```

The `trustver` field in the Provenance Declaration links the two systems.
